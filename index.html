Artist at the age of 27...

At the age of 27, Vincent van Gogh made a life-altering choice to pursue a career as an artist. Little did he know that this pivotal decision would not only shape his own destiny but also leave an indelible mark on the course of art history.
To explore the profound impact of Vincent's artistic journey and discover the transformative events that unfolded throughout his life, delve into the pages of Vincent's captivating biography.





Young Vincent's Life

1853 -1873

The Van Gogh family went on frequent walks in the area around Zundert, helping to instil a great love of nature in the future artist.





He was born at Zundert ...

Vincent van Gogh was born in the Brabant village of Zundert on 30 March 1853. He was not the first child of the Protestant minister Theodorus van Gogh and Anna Carbentus: another son (also named Vincent) had been stillborn on the same date, precisely one year earlier.
Happily, the birth of the second Vincent went smoothly, and was followed by that of three sisters and two brothers: Anna, Theo, Wil, Lies and Cor.






Schoolboy

At the tender age of eleven, Vincent made a significant transition from the village school in Zundert to a boarding school in Zevenbergen. Unfortunately, he found little solace at the new school, and though he managed to complete his elementary education, his time there was marked by profound unhappiness. While he occasionally engaged in drawing, there were no apparent signs of any exceptional artistic talent at that point.
At the age of thirteen, Vincent enrolled in a secondary school in Tilburg, where he displayed remarkable proficiency in languages and achieved commendable grades. Despite his academic success, for reasons unknown, he decided to abruptly leave the school halfway through his second year (1867-1868), and he never returned to formal education thereafter.




During the period of 1864-1866, Vincent attended a boarding school situated on Stationsstraat in Zevenbergen, with the white building on the left serving as the institution where he was a student.




Youngest clerk at an art dealer

At the age of sixteen, Vincent's uncle played a pivotal role in securing him a trainee position at the renowned international art dealer, Goupil & Cie. He was fortunate to be employed at the branch established by his affectionately nicknamed 'Uncle Cent' (short for 'Vincent') in The Hague. It was during these initial years at Goupil that Vincent wrote his earliest surviving letter.
The turning point came in September 1872 when Vincent's younger brother, Theo, initiated a lifelong exchange of letters between them. Theo, too, joined Goupil in 1873, but this time in Brussels. Subsequently, Vincent was transferred to Goupil's London branch in that same year. The Van Gogh brothers' association with Goupil would significantly shape their careers and influence the course of art history.


The first surviving letter from Vincent to his brother Theo: The Hague, 29 September 1872




Inspiration...

Theo was the manager of Goupil art dealers (later Boussod, Valadon & Cie) on the Boulevard Montmartre in Paris. He introduced his brother to the colourful work of prominent modern artists like Claude Monet. All those new impressions and new people had an influence on his own work and inspired him to experiment freely. The dark tones of The Potato Eaters quickly gave way to brighter colours, as in The Hill of Montmartre with Stone Quarry.

In Paris, Vincent developed his own, well-known style with bright colours. Yet after two years the hectic city life that had given him so much inspiration in the beginning, became too much.

Vincent's Final Months

1890

Vincent spent the final months of his life in Auvers-sur-Oise: an artists' village near Paris. He made new friends and he threw himself into painting. Yet after a visit to Theo, Vincent grew very concerned about his financial future. This uncertainty became too much to bear on top of his problems with his mental health. Vincent ended his life in July 1890.
